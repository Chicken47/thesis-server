# Indian Macroeconomic Context

_Last updated: 2026-02-25_

---

## 1. Monetary Policy

**Current Repo Rate:** 5.25% (held at February 2026 MPC meeting, February 6, 2026)

**RBI Stance:** Neutral-to-accommodative. RBI signalled rate stability amid inflation concerns while maintaining a focus on growth. No additional cuts are expected in the near term; a Reuters poll (January 28, 2026) projects rates steady at 5.25% through end-2026.

**Direction of Travel:** Cutting cycle is on pause after the December 2025 cut of 25 bps (from 5.50% to 5.25%). The February 2026 hold was a deliberate pause, not a reversal.

**Key Implications for Equity Markets:**

- Rate pause reduces immediate re-rating tailwind for rate-sensitive sectors (banking, NBFCs, real estate).
- Cost of capital remains stable; no further compression expected without a surprise cut.
- Stable rates are broadly constructive for earnings visibility across consumption and infrastructure sectors.
- Any future cut — if inflation stays subdued — could be a catalyst for mid/small-cap re-rating.

## 2. Inflation Environment

**CPI (January 2026):** 2.75% — steady and well within RBI's 2–6% tolerance band.

**WPI (January 2026):** 1.81% — a 10-month high (as of ABP Live, February 16, 2026), suggesting some upstream cost pressure building.

**Trend:** CPI has been comfortably low, enabling the December 2025 rate cut. WPI had hit a historic low of -1.21% in October 2025, driven by GST cuts and a favourable base, then ticked up to -0.32% in November 2025 and further to 1.81% in January 2026. The direction is upward but not alarming.

**Components:** Food price disinflation was a key driver of low WPI through mid-2025 (WPI hit a 2-year low in July 2025). The recent WPI uptick is being watched for pass-through to CPI.

**RBI Comfort Zone:** With CPI at 2.75%, RBI has headroom. However, the signal as of February 2026 is that the MPC is watching the inflation trajectory before committing to further cuts.

## 3. Growth Outlook

**Current Estimate:** India is projected to grow 6.8%–7.2% in FY2026–27 (Reuters/GoI, January 29, 2026). For FY2025–26, India Briefing projects 7.4% GDP growth (January 8, 2026). The UN projects 6.6% for calendar 2026, factoring in global headwinds.

**IMF View:** India's GDP is expected to grow faster than originally anticipated despite US tariff risks (October 2025).

**World Bank (South Asia Update, October 2025):** Constructive on India's medium-term trajectory.

**Risks Flagged (Reuters, January 29):** Geopolitical uncertainty, weak export demand.

**PMI / Capex / Consumption Signals:** Government capex front-loading is evident (capex up 32% through October 2025, per Business Standard, November 28, 2025). Public investment is compensating for softer private capex. Budget FY2027 targets capex at ₹12.2 lakh crore.

**Sectors Likely to Benefit:** Infrastructure, defence, capital goods, domestic consumption (aided by tax relief in Budget FY27). Sectors exposed to export demand and global macro volatility (IT, metals) face headwinds.

## 4. Liquidity & Credit

**Banking System Liquidity:** Supported by RBI's active Open Market Operations (OMOs). RBI absorbed nearly half of FY26 government borrowing via OMOs (Times of India, February 17, 2026), keeping system liquidity in manageable territory.

**Lenders' Requests:** Indian banks were petitioning RBI to ease liquidity rules ahead of the February 2026 policy meeting (Reuters, February 5, 2026), signalling the system has been operating under some tightness.

**Credit Growth Trends:** Economic Survey (January 29, 2026) backed RBI policy and flagged strong liquidity transmission. Detailed system credit growth numbers are not available in the headlines, but the overall signal is that transmission is working.

**Rate Transmission:** With the December 2025 25 bps cut, lending rate adjustments are in progress. The repo rate hold in February 2026 pauses further transmission for now. EMI impact from the December cut is working through floating-rate loans.

## 5. FII / DII Flows

**FII Trend:** Sustained net sellers. FII selling spree continued into 2026 after a record outflow year in 2025 (New Indian Express, January 11, 2026). On a sample day (January 14, 2026), FIIs sold ₹4,781 crore while DIIs bought ₹5,217 crore (scanx.trade).

**DII Counter-Flow:** DIIs are absorbing FII supply consistently. After 15 years, domestic institutions surpassed FPIs in ownership of NSE-listed stocks (Indian Express, May 2025). The domestic–foreign ownership gap hit a 25-year peak (Times of India, November 6, 2025).

**Drivers of FII Outflows:**

- Elevated US interest rates reducing EM risk appetite.
- Rupee depreciation reducing INR-denominated return attractiveness.
- Indian market's stretched valuations through mid-2025.
- Global uncertainty around US tariff policy and geopolitics.

**Key Watch:** Any reversal in DXY strength or Fed pivot signals could catalyse FII re-entry.

## 6. Currency & External Sector

**INR/USD Trend:** The rupee weakened substantially through 2025, hitting record lows. RBI intervened repeatedly to stabilise the currency (Bloomberg, November 3, 2025; December 17–18, 2025). Strategic intervention in February 2026 caused USD/INR to plunge (CryptoRank, February 12, 2026), suggesting active RBI management.

**RBI Posture:** Active intervener — not targeting a price band, but managing excessive volatility. The Times of India (January 9, 2026) noted RBI's approach reflects the "Impossible Trilemma" — prioritising inflation control and capital flow management over exchange rate fixity.

**Rupee Fundamentals:** Despite weak FII flows and import pressures, RBI intervention provided a floor. The rupee has seen intermittent recovery on central bank support (Reuters, October 20, 2025; Bloomberg, December 17, 2025).

**CAD / Forex Reserves:** _Insufficient data in headlines — monitor._ However, sustained FII outflows and crude oil import costs remain the primary CAD pressure points.

## 7. Commodity Context

**Crude Oil:** A key variable for India as a large importer.

- Brent crude was discussed as potentially crashing to $50/barrel in 2026 (Business Standard, December 12, 2025) — a bullish scenario for India's CAD and inflation.
- Alternatively, disruptions from US sanctions on Russian oil and OPEC dynamics could push Brent toward $80/barrel, pressuring Indian refiners (Times of India, August 2, 2025).
- US tariffs on India and US crude stock movements have created oil price volatility (Reuters, August 27, 2025).
- Trump's Russia oil sanctions add geopolitical risk to supply (CNBC, October 23, 2025).

**Impact on India:** Lower crude is structurally positive — reducing inflation pressure, narrowing the CAD, and benefiting downstream consumers and aviation. Higher crude does the opposite and pressures OMC margins.

**Other Commodities:** WPI data suggests agri commodity prices have been a disinflationary force through most of H2 FY25. _Metals data insufficient — monitor._

## 8. Fiscal Policy

**FY27 Budget (Presented February 1, 2026):**

- Fiscal Deficit Target: 4.3% of GDP (NDTV, February 1, 2026)
- Capital Expenditure: ₹12.2 lakh crore
- Government is maintaining fiscal consolidation while supporting growth through capex.

**FY26 Execution:**

- Fiscal deficit reached 52.6% of Budget Estimate by October 2025, with capex up 32% (Business Standard, November 28, 2025).
- By January 2026, India had already spent 62% of its fiscal deficit target (Goodreturns, January 5, 2026) — tracking broadly in line; not a red flag at this stage.
- H1 FY26 deficit held up as Centre ramped public investment (Mint, October 31, 2025).

**Key Spending Themes:** Infrastructure (roads, railways), defence, PLI-linked manufacturing, and rural/social spend. The Budget FY27 maintains this orientation.

**Fiscal Risk:** With 62% of deficit already consumed pre-Budget, execution discipline in H2 FY26 is important. No major fiscal slippage signal visible in the data.

## 9. SEBI & Regulatory Environment

**Securities Market Code, 2025 (Bill No. 200 of 2025):** Major legislative overhaul of Indian securities law, analysed by Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas (January 7, 2026). Signals a comprehensive modernisation of the regulatory framework.

**Enforcement Actions:**

- SEBI ordered disgorgement of ₹546 crore in "unlawful gains" against Avadhut Sathe Trading Academy (New Indian Express, December 5, 2025) — largest finfluencer crackdown on record.
- SEBI ordered recovery proceedings of ₹18+ crore against finfluencer "Baap of Chart" (MediaNama, December 17, 2025).
- Tata Capital paid ₹14.40 lakh to settle an unlisted CRPS distribution case (Moneylife, December 3, 2025).
- 360 One Private Equity Fund settled a case for ₹14.62 lakh (Moneycontrol, August 28, 2025).
- Godrej Agrovet received a SEBI warning over Astec Lifesciences acquisition disclosures (scanx.trade, September 29, 2025).
- SEBI launched NSEL Settlement Scheme for broker relief (HDFC Sky, July 9, 2025).

**Regulatory Themes:** Aggressive enforcement against finfluencers and unregistered investment advice; push to modernise the statutory framework via the Securities Market Code; settlement mechanism being used actively to resolve legacy disputes.

**Regulatory Risk to Watch:** Compliance burden from the new Securities Market Code as it passes into law; continued scrutiny of social media-driven investment activity.

## 10. Overall Market Positioning

**Nifty/Sensex Context:**

- Indian equities were branded the "world's worst" in 2025 by some metrics (Economic Times, September 18, 2025), after significant underperformance driven by FII outflows and slowing earnings growth.
- Markets recovered, scaling new 14-month highs in November 2025 (Reuters, November 27, 2025) on growth optimism and easing valuations.
- Consensus as of end-2025/early 2026: double-digit returns possible in 2026, with select sectors (pure-play domestic consumption, infra) leading (Upstox, December 10, 2025).

**Key Tailwinds:**

- Benign inflation enabling an accommodative policy backdrop.
- Strong DII flows providing market support.
- Government capex driving infrastructure and capital goods earnings.
- Rate cut cycle (even if paused) reducing cost of capital vs. the FY24 peak.
- INR stabilisation reducing FII currency-risk hesitancy.

**Key Risks:**

- Sustained FII selling on global risk-off or USD strength.
- Crude oil spike (Russia sanctions, OPEC) widening CAD and pressuring margins.
- Global slowdown dampening export-oriented sectors.
- Geopolitical uncertainty (US tariff escalation on India flagged in multiple Reuters/ET reports).
- Execution miss on fiscal consolidation or capex deployment.
- Regulatory disruption from the Securities Market Code transition.
